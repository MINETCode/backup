Round One ·

Romans

Given a number, convert it into its Roman form.
If you’re not familiar with the Roman system, have a look at the appendix.

Input: A natural number $n$

Output: The Roman equivalent of $n$

Limit: $1 \leq n \leq 3000$

Example

Input Output
2014 MMXIV

Appendix

Symbol I V X L C D M
Value 1 5 10 50 100 500 1000

Numbers are formed by combining symbols and adding the values, so II is two (two ones) and XIII is thirteen (a ten and three ones). There is no zero in this system and characters do not represent tens, hundreds and so on according to position as in 207 or 1066; those numbers are written as CCVII (two hundreds, a five and two ones) and MLXVI (a thousand, a fifty, a ten, a five and a one). Symbols are placed from left to right in order of value, starting with the largest. However, in a few specific cases, to avoid four characters being repeated in succession (such as IIII or XXXX), subtractive notation is often used as follows: I placed before V or X indicates one less, so four is IV (one less than five) and nine is IX (one less than ten) X placed before L or C indicates ten less, so forty is XL (ten less than fifty) and ninety is XC (ten less than a hundred) C placed before D or M indicates a hundred less, so four hundred is CD (a hundred less than five hundred) and nine hundred is CM (a hundred less than a thousand) For example, MCMIV is one thousand nine hundred and four, 1904 (M is a thousand, CM is nine hundred and IV is four). So 1954 is MCMLIV.

Totally Antisymmetric

Let us generalise matrices $A_{ij}$ to higher dimensional analogues called tensors $T_{ijkl…}$ so that the number of indices is called the rank of the tensor and indices run from 1 to $o$, where $o$ is called the order of the tensor. Let us consider the ‘totally antisymmetric tensor $E$ of rank $r$ and order $r$ that has the following properties:

  • Every element can have value equal to one of $1$, $0$ and $-1$.
  • $E_{123…r} = 1$.
  • Whenever you exchange two of its indices, the sign reverses. $E_{…i…j…} = - E_{…j…i…}$

Notice (try to prove!) that if any one index is repeated, $E$ is zero. $E_{…i…i…} = 0$ for all $1 \leq i \leq r$. You have to compute $E_{ijk…}$ for any given i, j, k, …

Input: The first line gives $r$, the rank of the tensor $E$. The next $r$ lines give r numbers $i, j, k, ….$ where all the indices are less than or equal to $r$.

Output: $E_{ijk…}$

Limits: $2 \leq r \leq 4200$

Example

Input Output
3
1
3
2		 1

Moving a Knight

The knight move is unusual among chess pieces. When it moves, it can move to a square that is two squares horizontally and one square vertically, or two squares vertically and one square horizontally. The complete move therefore looks like the letter L. A chessboard is labelled 1-8 and a-h along the rows and columns respectively. Suppose a knight starts at the square a1. You have to print the moves to take it to a given square.

Input: Two lines of input, the first containing the column alphabet and the second containing the row number.

Output: The moves that take a knight from a1 to the given square.

Example

Input Output
a2 b3 c1 a2

Peaceful Rooks

Let n be an integer. Consider a chessboard consisting of $n\times n$ unit squares. A configuration of $n$ rooks on this board is called (for an obvious reason) peaceful if every row and every column contains exactly one rook. Find the greatest positive integer $k$ such that, for each peaceful configuration of n rooks, there is a square which does not contain a rook on any of its $k \times k$ squares.

Input: An integer $n$

Output: The greatest possible $k$ as described in the problem statement

Limit: $2 \leq n \leq 10000$

Example

Input Output
4 1

Peaceful Queens

In a game of chess, a queen is able to move any number of squares in an orthogonal direction (up, down, left or right) or diagonally and can capture any piece that blocks its line of movement. A peaceful configuration of queens is one in which no queen threatens any other. Given $n$, find the number of possible peaceful configurations of $n$ queens on an $n\times n$ chessboard.

Input: An integer $n$.

Output: The number of peaceful configurations of $n$ queens on an $n\times n$ chessboard.

Limits: $2 \leq n \leq 12$

Happyland

The country of Happyland has very strict laws governing how companies organize and pay their employees:

  1. Each company must have exactly one CEO, who has no manager.
  2. Every employee except for the CEO must have exactly one manager. (This means that the org chart showing all of the employees in a company is a tree, without cycles.)
  3. As long as an employee is working for the company, their manager must never change. This means that if a manager leaves, then all of the employees reporting to that manager must also leave.
  4. The CEO must never leave the company.
  5. Every employee receives a salary — some amount of Happyland dollars per year. An employee's salary must never change.
  6. Different employees may have different salaries, and an employee's salary is not necessarily correlated with where in the org chart that employee is.

The government of Happyland has just passed one additional law:

  1. The difference between the largest salary and the smallest salary in the whole company must be at most $D$ Happyland dollars.

Marie is the CEO of the Happyland General Stuff Corporation, and she has to ensure that her company complies with the new law. This may require laying off some employees. She has the list of the company's employees, their managers, and their salaries. What is the largest number of employees she can keep, including herself?

Input: One line containing two space-separated integers $N$ (the number of employees) and $D$ (the maximum allowed salary difference). This is followed by one line with four space-separated integers ($A_0, A_s, C_s, R_s$) and then another line with four more space-separated integers ($M_0, A_m, C_m and R_m$). These last eight integers define the following sequences:

  • $S_{i+1} = (S_i \times A_s + C_s)$ mod $R_s$
  • $M_{i+1} = (M_i \times A_m + C_m)$ mod $R_m$

Marie's employee ID is 0, and all other employees have IDs from 1 to $N - 1$, inclusive. The salary of employee $i$ is $S_i$. For every employee $i$ other than Marie, the manager is $M_i$ mod $i$. (Note that this means that $M_0$ does not affect Marie's manager -- she has none!)

Output: For each test case, output one y, where y is the largest number of employees Marie can keep at the company, including herself, such that all of laws 1-7 are obeyed.

Limits: $0 \leq $A_s, A_m$ \leq 1000$ and $0 \leq $A_s, A_m$ \leq 10^9$

Enigma

Some teachers were inspired by the movie 'The Imitation Game' which was loosely based on the professional and personal history of Alan Turing. A significant portion of the movie revolves around cracking the code generated by Enigma machine which was employed by the German Army. As a joke, these teachers created an Enigma machine of their own to send secret messages to their fellow teachers. As students were the physical messengers of these messages, who lugged around carrying sheets of papers, encryption was the way to go.

For those who are not familiar with the concept of the Enigma machine, please check the Appendix at the end of this question before proceeding with the question.

One of the students decided to crack the code. He snuck into one of the teachers’ room, and found the Enigma machine lying unguarded on the table. It only had 2 rotors with 26 numbers on each rotor (0 to 25). Seizing the opportunity he noted down all the numbers in the order.

1st Rotor: [16, 10, 13, 17, 11, 6, 4, 7, 18, 19, 2, 20, 12, 9, 1, 24, 22, 15, 8, 21, 23, 14, 5, 25, 3, 0]
2nd Rotor: [22, 9, 1, 3, 17, 8, 23, 2, 6, 18, 16, 0, 25, 14, 4, 10, 19, 5, 21, 20, 24, 7, 13, 15, 12, 11]

The principal of the school has a habit of greeting everyone with "good morning everyone", which indicated them to be the principal’s first words in the first message.

The starting point of the rotors for each day was a secret which was known to all the teachers beforehand.

Today's first words were an easy catch as that student was taking the message himself.

Message: good morning everyone
Encrypted: qlnt tpwpazv ximxhiii

Your task is to figure out today's starting point using a C++ program and the given information. Also, write a program to decrypt the messages using the starting point.

Input: The encrypted message as a string of characters (only lowercase characters)

Output: The decrypted message

Example

Input Output
wbdjpol cl zdha qt cbz wpoqi rmfa meeting at noon in the staff room

Appendix

The enigma machine generates pseudo-random shifts for each character in the message. In this case 2 rotors are used with random numbers (0 to 25) on the rotor disks. Let us take a toy example to understand how the rotor system works

1st Rotor: [0, 1, 2]
2nd Rotor: [0, 1, 2]

Let the starting position be (0,1) and the message be 'abc xyz'. The rotor will operate in the following manner

1st Rotor 2nd Rotor Character Shift Message Encrypted
0 1 0 + 1 = 1 a b
1 1 1 + 1 = 2 b d
2 1 2 + 1 = 3 c f
0 2 0 + 2 = 2 x z
1 2 1 + 2 = 3 y b
2 2 2 + 2 = 4 z d
0 0 0 + 0 = 0    
1 0 1 + 0 = 1    
2 0 2 + 0 = 2    
... ... ... ... ...

and so on
Hence,
Encrypted Message = ‘bdf zbd’

Note

  • No shift is applied on white spaces
  • The message can only consist of 26 alphabets, hence a shift of 1 on 'z' will take you back to the start and yield the encrypted character to be 'a'.

Towers of Hanoi

The Tower of Hanoi puzzle was invented by the French mathematician Edouard Lucas in 1883. He was inspired by a legend that tells of a Hindu temple where the puzzle was presented to young priests. At the beginning of time, the priests were given three poles and a stack of 64 gold disks, each disk a little smaller than the one beneath it. Their assignment was to transfer all 64 disks from one of the three poles to another, with two important constraints. They could only move one disk at a time, and they could never place a larger disk on top of a smaller one. The priests worked very efficiently, day and night, moving one disk every second. When they finished their work, the legend said, the temple would crumble into dust and the world would vanish. Although the legend is interesting, you need not worry about the world ending any time soon. The number of moves required to correctly move a tower of 64 disks is, in fact, 18,446,744,073,709,551,615. At a rate of one move per second, that is 584,942,417,355 years! Clearly there is more to this puzzle than meets the eye. We want you to solve the puzzle for n disks. Supposing there are n disks at pole A, print the moves required to move them to pole C with the constraints we mentioned.

Input: The number of disks at pole A at the beginning of the game

Output: Valid moves to move them to pole C in the format given in the example.

Limits: $1\leq n\leq 64$

Example

Input Output
2 1 A to B
2 A to C
3 B to C

Game of Life

In Conway's Game of Life, cells in a grid are used to simulate biological cells. Each cell is considered to be either alive or dead. At each step of the simulation each cell's current status and number of living neighbors is used to determine the status of the cell during the following step of the simulation. In this one-dimensional version, there are $N$ cells numbered $0$ through $N-1$. The number of cells does not change at any point in the simulation. Each cell $i$ is adjacent to cells $i-1$ and $i+1$. Here, the indices are taken modulo $N$ meaning cells $0$ and $N-1$ are also adjacent to eachother. At each step of the simulation, cells with exactly one living neighbor change their status (alive cells become dead, dead cells become alive). For example, if we represent dead cells with a '0' and living cells with a '1', consider the state with 8 cells:

01100101

  • Cells 0 and 6 have two living neighbors.
  • Cells 1, 2, 3, and 4 have one living neighbor.
  • Cells 5 and 7 have no living neighbors.

Thus, at the next step of the simulation, the state would be:
00011101
Given some state of the game, your task is to determine the state immediately preceding it. In some cases there may be more than one answer or no possible answer.

Input: Input will begin with an integer $t$, the number of test cases. Each test case consists of a single line.

Output: For each test case, output the state of the game that precedes the given state. If there is no possible solution, print "No solution". If there are multiple possible solutions, print "Multiple solutions".

Limits: $1\leq t \leq 100$. Each test case can be from 3 to 20 characters long.

Example

Input Output
3
00011101
000001
11110		 01100101
No solution
